How to use this tool
A step-by-step guide to sizing your contact centre workforce accurately

What does this tool do?
The RFP Sizing Calculator helps you calculate the number of Full-Time Equivalents (FTE) needed to handle a given contact volume across multiple locations and channels. It accounts for real-world factors like shrinkage, shift patterns, and operating hours — so your headcount estimates are grounded in operational reality, not just raw workload maths.
📞
Phone
Volume in calls/month with AHT in seconds or minutes. Supports HOOP shift bands and peak/off-peak splits.
💬
Chat
Same as phone, plus a concurrency multiplier (default 1.2) which divides the FTE requirement by the average simultaneous chats per agent.
✉️
Email
Async channel. Supports a target response SLA % and a backlog buffer to account for queued work beyond the day's arrivals.
📊
Summary
Cross-channel view showing all locations, workload hours, attendance hours, FTE, and monthly contracted hours in one table.
💡
This tool does not save data automatically. Always copy or screenshot your results before leaving the page.

Locations & Volume Mode
The tool supports up to 5 locations across Philippines, India, South Africa, USA, and UK. Each location carries its own contracted hours/month, shift length, and shrinkage profile. You control how volume is distributed using the toggle in the top-right of each channel tab.
  • 1
    ⚖ Equal volumes
    The same total volume is sent to every active location. Use this when you haven't yet decided on a location split. Important: deselect locations that are not in scope using the ✓ toggle on their card — otherwise their FTE will be overcounted.
  • 2
    ↗ Split volumes
    Assign a percentage of total volume to each location (must sum to 100%). Use this when you have a defined routing strategy, e.g. 60% PH / 40% IN.
⚠️
In Equal volumes mode, all 5 locations receive the full volume by default. Always deactivate locations not in scope — the amber banner at the top of the channel view is your reminder.
📉
Shrinkage — Utilisation & Availability
Shrinkage accounts for the gap between an agent being scheduled and being available to handle contacts. It is split into two components, both editable on each location card.
  • 1
    Utilisation % (default 10%)
    Time lost to non-productive scheduled activities — team meetings, coaching, training, system downtime. Applied first in the formula: Attendance hrs = Workload hrs ÷ (1 − Util)
  • 2
    Availability % (default 20%)
    Time lost to unplanned absence — sick leave, attrition buffer, late arrivals. Applied second: Attendance hrs ÷ (1 − Availability)
  • 3
    Combined Shrinkage
    At defaults (10% + 20%), total shrinkage = ~28%. Shown in the top-right pill of the header. Click any % field on a location card to edit it.
💡
Industry benchmarks: Utilisation 8–15%, Availability 15–25%. Higher shrinkage = more FTE required. Lowering shrinkage improves efficiency on paper but increases risk.
🕐
HOOP — Hours of Operation
The HOOP panel (expandable below each channel's input strip) lets you define when your operation runs. This converts the base workload FTE into a unique headcount that accounts for shift rotation and coverage patterns.
  • 1
    Operating days & hours
    Select which days of the week are staffed and set open/close times. Use the 24/7 shortcut for round-the-clock operations.
  • 2
    Shift rotation multiplier
    If your operating window is longer than the country's standard shift (e.g. 12h window ÷ 9h PH shift = 2 rotations), the tool automatically multiplies FTE to give the correct unique headcount.
  • 3
    Weekday / Weekend splits
    Enter the % of volume handled on weekdays vs weekends. The tool calculates each band's FTE independently and sums to a total unique headcount. Required for 24/7 operations.
  • 4
    Peak / Off-peak splits
    For operations with a defined busy period (e.g. 70% peak, 30% off-peak), these bands are staffed independently with a minimum of 3 FTE per active band.
⚠️
For 24/7 coverage with weekend staffing, always enter Weekday % and Weekend % — the tool will prompt you if these are missing.
👥
FTE vs Unique Headcount — What's the Difference?
This is one of the most important distinctions in workforce sizing. The two numbers answer different questions.
📋
Base FTE (workload)
How many full-time agents' worth of work exists, accounting for shrinkage. This is the minimum if everyone worked the same shift with no rotation.
🧑‍🤝‍🧑
Unique Headcount (HOOP)
How many individual people you need to hire, once you account for shift rotations, band splits, and minimum staffing floors. Always ≥ base FTE.
  • !
    When to use which
    Use Base FTE for capacity planning and commercial proposals. Use Unique Headcount for hiring plans, seat counts, and operational budgeting. When an amber warning appears on a card, it means unique headcount exceeds base FTE — hire to the higher number.
💡
Example: 660 calls/month, 5.5 min AHT, Mon–Fri 08:00–20:00 at a PH site → Base FTE = 1, but 12h window ÷ 9h shift = 2 rotations → Unique HC = 2. You cannot staff a 12-hour window with one person.
🔢
Erlang C — Queue-Based Staffing
The 🔢 Erlang C tab provides a second, independent view of your staffing requirement using a queuing model — more appropriate for real-time channels like phone and chat.
Why Erlang C gives a higher FTE than simple workload
Simple workload assumes contacts arrive smoothly and evenly. In reality, calls arrive randomly. Erlang C models this randomness and calculates the agents needed so that X% of contacts are answered within Y seconds (your service level target). It typically adds 10–30% more agents than workload FTE.
  • 1
    Set your service level target
    Default is 80% of contacts answered within 20 seconds — a common industry benchmark. Adjust to match your SLA.
  • 2
    Occupancy is a calculated output
    Erlang C calculates occupancy (A/N) for you — it is not an input. The Occ Cap is a safety ceiling. Raising it allows fewer agents (higher burnout risk); lowering it forces more agents.
  • 3
    Use Erlang C for commercial SLA commitments
    If you're bidding on a contract with a defined SLA (e.g. "80/20"), use the Erlang C FTE in your commercial model, not the workload FTE. The delta vs workload is shown on each location card.
📧
Email does not queue — Erlang C does not apply. The Email card in Erlang C view uses a workload + SL-buffer approach as a conservative estimate only.

Quick Start Checklist
Follow these steps to get an accurate sizing in under 5 minutes.
  • 1
    Select your volume mode
    ⚖ Equal — if you haven't decided on location split yet. ↗ Split — if you have a defined routing strategy.
  • 2
    Deactivate out-of-scope locations
    In equal mode, click the ✓ on location cards to deactivate any country not being staffed. This prevents their volume from being counted.
  • 3
    Enter volume & AHT for each channel
    Use the strip at the top of each tab. Volume can be monthly or annual. AHT can be seconds (phone default) or minutes.
  • 4
    Set HOOP operating window
    Expand the HOOP panel and set operating days + hours. Add weekday/weekend splits if relevant. This unlocks unique headcount.
  • 5
    Review warnings
    Amber banners mean you need to take action — e.g. deselect locations, add splits, or hire to unique HC instead of base FTE.
  • 6
    Check Erlang C for SLA-based sizing
    If the contract has a defined service level (e.g. 80/20), switch to the Erlang C tab and verify your FTE against the queue model.
  • 7
    Export from Summary tab
    The 📊 Summary tab shows all channels and locations in one table. Screenshot or copy this for your RFP response.

Erlang C — Agent Requirements
Queue-based staffing model with service level constraint
ℹ️ About Erlang C: Unlike simple workload sizing, Erlang C models random call arrivals and queue wait times. It calculates the minimum agents needed so that X% of contacts are answered within Y seconds. Occupancy (A/N) is a calculated output — the model adds agents until both the SL target and the occupancy cap are satisfied. Raising the cap allows fewer agents but higher burnout risk; lowering it forces more agents. Results are typically 10–30% higher than workload FTE. Note: Erlang C applies to Phone and Chat (queued channels). Email uses a workload + SL-buffer approach.
